# Motivation and Scope

## Why Study Quantum Field Theory?

Quantum field theory stands as one of physics' greatest intellectual achievements, providing the theoretical foundation for our understanding of fundamental particles and forces. Its importance extends far beyond academic interest, with practical applications in:

### Technological Impact

Modern technology relies heavily on quantum field theoretical principles:

- **Semiconductors** - Band structure calculations using quantum many-body theory
- **Quantum Computing** - Manipulation of quantum states and entanglement
- **Medical Imaging** - Positron emission tomography exploits electron-positron annihilation

### Fundamental Questions

Several deep questions motivate our study:

1. **Unity of Forces** - How do electromagnetic, weak, and strong interactions relate?
2. **Quantum Gravity** - Can general relativity be quantized consistently?
3. **Information Paradox** - What happens to information in black holes?

### Mathematical Foundations
We develop the canonical formalism starting from classical field equations. The Klein-Gordon equation for a scalar field $\phi$ serves as our prototype:

$$\left( \partial_\mu \partial^\mu + m^2 \right) \phi = 0$$

Through canonical quantization, this becomes an operator equation with commutation relations:

$$[\phi(\mathbf{x}), \pi(\mathbf{y})] = i \hbar \delta^3(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y})$$

where $\pi = \partial \mathcal{L}/\partial \dot{\phi}$ is the conjugate momentum field.

### Physical Applications
We examine how theoretical principles manifest in observable phenomena, particularly focusing on black hole physics and thermodynamic behavior.
